10 Things About Blockchain


1) Who protects data?


Traditional technology has 3rd parties own, protect and manage information. (Equifax, Target) We as society give them our data and trust them to secure it with one key that they hold. 3rd parties essentially own the data and hold the key to that data.

Blockchain flips this process. It puts data onto a public blockchain for anyone to see. Keys to use the data is not held by a 3rd party. Instead the keys are kept by the users. Essentially, everyone sees the data, but each individual has their own key to use only their data.

The old system had a huge amount of data protected by one very important secure key held by a 3rd party.

The blockchain has each person hold the keys to their own data.

2) Passwords and Keys


A password is a user generated string of charectors that is easy to remember. They are often easy for a computer to guess.

A key is a really long string of charectors that is difficult to remember or enter into a website. But, it is also ultra secure. It is what the blockchain uses.

The blockchain cannot "remind or reset" a password. There is no 3rd party to help you. This is super important. Protect your blockchain keys. If someone else has the private key- they can access your data. If you lose/forget your key - it is lost forever.

3) Blockchain Nodes


The blockchain is made up of nodes. Each node has a copy of the blockchain. Each node can add transactions to the blockchain and get information from the blockchain. For Ethereum a node is typically run on Geth (Geth stands for Go Ethereum - "go" is the programming language it is written in)

A node has cryptography built into it to protect the keys of the person running the node. This allows a user to interact with blockchain using keys without having to remember the keys. They just need to run their node - and keep their computer safe.

The Ethereum Foundation created a program called "Web3" to interact with a node. Web3 is a javascript program that makes it easy for developers to run commands on a node. Without Web3 users would need to type low level commands into a terminal window. Web3 lets developers create usable apps and sites for you to enjoy.

The Ethereum blockchain's browser is called "Mist". Mist includes "Web3" which is connected to a local node running Geth.

4) FinTechToken Shared Node


If our site detects a local instance of Web3 we will use it. We work with Mist.

However, we also created a solution to make getting on blockchain both easy and secure for customers who don't want to install Geth/Mist/Web3.

We feel getting users to run local nodes is a big ask. We also feel it isn't practical over the long term. The Ethereum blockchain is over 20gigs. This means a user would need to keep 20gigs on their machine, and their machine would need to process transactions which uses computing power and bandwidth.

We created a shared node hosted on Amazon Web Services. The shared node does NOT manage keys and user accounts like a typical local node. We moved account and key management off the node and into our website. The website is written in Angular and runs on the clients browser.

This means the keys/encryption is not done on our server. It stays locally with the user. However, the user doesn't need Geth/Mist/or Web3.

5) Authentication


We do three things to authenticate and protect users on the shared node.

1) We encrypt your key with a password you create. We call this your "encrypted id". The encrypted id stores your private key.

We do this so our users can authenticate with a usable password - yet have the security of a complex key.

It is best practice to mix up 4 or 5 random words for a password. This is easy to remember if you create a story about the words. But super hard for a computer to crack. For example "Pink Ground Piano Climbing Rock"

2) Keys never touch our site and are never stored on your browser.
We also do not send keys over the wire. Keys are used on your browser to encrypt a message that our site forwards to the blockchain.

3) We do not store encrypted ids on our site. Your encrypted id is stored in your browsers storage secured so only our site can use it.

6) Token Market Smart Contract


A smart contract is code. Unlike a legal contract written by lawyers - a smart contract is written by programmers.

Users of Ethereum have addresses where they hold digital assets like Ether and interact with the blockchain. Smart contracts also get an address to hold digital assets and interact with the blockchain.

Once published - a smart contracts code cannot change. This is enormously different than traditional programs where software can be patched or updated. It's also different from traditional law where legal contracts can be amended.

Legal contracts are enforced by judges and the legal system. This enforcement may be costly, timely, and potentially corrupt process. Smart contracts are enforced by the ethereum network. The code will do exactly what it is programmed to do.

This means a smart contract may have mistakes in it. Just like a lawyer can make costly mistakes by ommiting a critical line in a contract. In the same way a programmer may have unintended consequences in a smart contract.

The Token Market is a smart contract. Assets are never held, nor can they be touched by us, a third party, or anyone. Assets are kept on the smart contract and we do not have custody.

7) Private blockchain


Smart contracts cannot be hacked. All Ethereum hacks have either been due to bad code, or have been off blockchain issues with 3rd parties.

Our token market is beta software and we want to protect you from bad code. To protect users we did two things.

1) We created a kill command. This command can only be issued by us - it prevents all transactions except withdrawing digital assets from the token market.

2) We created a private blockchain. The private chain runs exactly like the Ethereum network. However, it is not connected to the real blockchain.

When you use our private chain you risk nothing. We don't use real ether, or real digital assets.

8) Transfer


The Token Marketplace protects users by only allowing users to trade assets they move onto the Token Market smart contract. This means users are guaranteed that the other party to a transaction has the funds to complete the transaction. It also means your assets are protected since there is no 3rd party to lose or touch your funds. The Smart Contract is the 3rd party.

Deposit into the Token Market
You can deposit Ether or ERC20 tokens/smart contracts onto the Token Market for free. When transferred the address that sent the assets is your account. Withdrawal out of the Token Market
At any point in time you can transfer Ether or ERC20 tokens/smart contracts off the Token Market smart contract for free. This means you can get your funds at any time.

Get Ether or get out of Ether

Users can transfer from fiat or another blockchain into Ether. They can also transfer from Ether to fiat or another blockchain.

Fiat is traditional currency like USD, EUR, YEN, RNB. Examples of other blockchains are BitCoin, Litecoin, and ripple.

9) Trade


ERC20 Smart Contracts exchanged for Ether
The market lets users offer to buy/sell any ERC20 smart contracts for Ether. Everything is priced in Ether.

What is an ERC20 Contract?
ERC20 is a standard. The standard means a smart contract has certain functions. The function we need is called "transfer". The Token Market needs that function to transfer smart contracts between accounts.

Make an offer
Once you deposit Ether or ERC20 Smart contracts onto the Token Market; you can make an offer to buy/sell ERC20 Smart contracts. Simply state how much Ether you want to pay for a smart contract and how many smart contracts you want to buy. Or how much Ether you want to receive to sell a smart contract and how many you want to sell.

Settlement
If someone else already made an offer that meets your offer a transaction will occur immediately. If you are selling tokens - you will get Ether and the other party will get the tokens. If you are buying tokens - you will get the Tokens and the other party will get the Ether. The transaction takes place at the price that already existed on the book.

Offer on the book
If there is not an existing offer that meets your offer, your offer goes on the book. This means it is publicly shared and if another party makes an offer equal to or better than yours a transaction will occur at your price. When an offer goes on the book the assets is removed from your account and place on the book. This ensures assets exist for all transactions.

Removing an offer
If you have an offer on the book you may cancel it at any time prior to a transaction occuring. This means you pull the offer off the book and the assets go back into your account.

View Book
The book is public. This means everyone can see what the current best offer is - what all other offers are - what previous transactions occured - and what accounts are making offers and transacting. A book exists for all ERC20 smart contracts.

10) Gas


Gas is used to pay the network to execute smart contract code. Gas is paid by the person executing a transaction. If you want to make an offer - you pay the gas to execute the make an offer function.

When you ask a smart contract to do something you choose how much you are willing to pay for gas, and the most amount of gas you are willing to use. The idea is people who want transactions to happen immediately can pay more for gas, than people who are not in a rush.

Users can pick how much to bid for gas, and the max amount of gas to use. We will tell users an estimate of the gas amount and potential tips to lower the amount of gas used.

This may be too complex for some users. As such we also will allow users to pick fast, medium, slow and let our site make bids for gas based on recent transaction on the blockchain.

There are two main problems with gas and having a book on blockchain.

1) If I want to buy 100 tokens - and someone else wants to sell 100 tokens there are 2 writes to the database which costs 40k in gas. This is fine. What if I want to buy 100 tokens - and 100 other people want to sell 1 token each. Now there would be 200 writes to the database. This would cost 4M in gas. This means when making an offer the cost could vary by a factor of 100.

Our solution is that people place 40k in gas worth of Ether as part of their offer if it goes on the book. If you cancel your transaction you get it back. But, if someone hits your offer they get the Ether. This means that the person who paid 4M in gas for 100 sellers would receive Ether worth about 4M in gas.

2) Searching the book. Lets say people make offers at 1 cent increments. If there is $1 in offers there will be 100 offers to search. Each search costs about 1k in gas. 100 offers would be 100K in gas. $100 dollar spread would be about 10,000 items which would be 10M gas to search all of them. The max gas in a transaction is curerntly 6M. Plus, that would be fairly expensive.

We created an algorithm that exponentially lowers the cost for searching a long list. This algorithm theoretically can search 10,000 items for under 500k in gas. One of the reasons we want users to try our test network is to test and improve this algorithm.
